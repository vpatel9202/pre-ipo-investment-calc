03 / 15PRIMER
For physicians, not physicists

Fusion in 90 seconds.

Two hydrogen atoms slammed together fuse into helium. The new helium atom weighs slightly less than the sum of its parts. The missing mass becomes energy. This is what powers the sun.

To do it on Earth, you need to:

  • Heat hydrogen to ~150 million °C — ten times hotter than the sun's core.
  • Hold that hot, charged gas (plasma) in place long enough to fuse, without it touching anything physical.
  • Capture the energy released — fast neutrons heating a blanket, blanket heating water, water turning a turbine. The back end is conventional power-plant engineering.

The hard part is the middle. A plasma at 150M°C will instantly destroy any material vessel. So you contain it without touching it — using magnetic fields strong enough to suspend the plasma in a vacuum chamber. The shape of the chamber and field geometry defines the fusion approach: tokamak (donut), stellarator (twisted donut), Z-pinch (column), inertial (laser implosion), and others.

The unsolved threshold

"Q greater than 1"

Q is the ratio of fusion energy out to heating energy in. Q>1 means the plasma is producing more than it consumes. Q>10 is roughly what a power plant needs.

In December 2022, the National Ignition Facility's laser approach briefly crossed Q>1 in a single shot, lasting nanoseconds. No machine has ever produced sustained, commercially relevant net energy. CFS's SPARC is one of three projects worldwide attempting this in the next 24 months.

The fusion product (n·T·τ) — density × temperature × confinement time — is the single number that captures the problem. Every fusion company is just a different bet on which combination is achievable in a buildable machine.

04 / 15THE THESIS
CFS's specific bet

Better magnets, smaller machine.

Fusion power density scales as B⁴ — the fourth power of the magnetic field strength. Doubling the field gives sixteen times the fusion power per unit volume. This is the entire CFS thesis.

The conventional path

ITER, the international fusion mega-project under construction in France, uses low-temperature superconducting magnets at ~12 Tesla. To produce useful fusion power at that field strength requires a machine the size of an apartment building, costing $40–60B, with first plasma now targeted for 2035.

It is the most validated physics in fusion. It is also slow, expensive, and not commercial.

The CFS path

Use rare-earth barium copper oxide (REBCO) high-temperature superconductors, which sustain ~20 Tesla in a far smaller volume. Same physics; one-tenth the size. SPARC is roughly the size of a tennis court. ARC, the commercial successor, fits on a coal-plant footprint.

In September 2021, CFS and MIT demonstrated a 20T REBCO magnet at scale.1 The bet was technically validated. Everything since is engineering the rest of the machine around the magnet.

The translated stakes

If the magnet thesis works at the system level, fusion power plants can be built in the cost and timeline envelope of conventional thermal power plants — not aerospace mega-projects. That is the difference between a curiosity and an industry. It is also the difference between a venture-scale return and a research grant.

06 / 15FOR INVESTOR A'S SCIENCE SKEPTICISM (2/2)
Honest accounting of what isn't known

What is still speculative.

The science risks separate cleanly into SPARC risks (resolved or not by 2027) and ARC risks (resolved or not by 2032+). The first set is real but bounded. The second set is structurally harder.

Near-term — SPARC risks

1. Confinement scaling at 12 T is extrapolated, not measured. H-mode access threshold and energy confinement time at this field have never been observed at scale. Surprises in confinement scaling have, historically, been unfavorable. A 30% miss on heating power requirements is plausible.

2. Disruption physics at high field. A sudden plasma disruption can quench the magnets. REBCO quench propagation is slower and less forgiving than ITER-class low-temperature superconductors. A magnet damage event would mean a 6–12 month repair and a narrative break.

3. Tritium runtime. Global civilian tritium supply is ~25 kg, mostly Canadian CANDU reactors.10 SPARC will run weeks of D-T operation per year, slowing the empirical learning loop. This is a logistical, not physical, ceiling.

Long-term — ARC risks

1. Tritium breeding ratio (TBR). ARC needs to breed more tritium than it consumes (TBR > 1.05) using a liquid FLiBe blanket surrounding the plasma. No fusion blanket has ever operated anywhere. Published designs cluster uncomfortably close to 1.0. If ARC's geometry comes back at TBR 0.95, the plant cannot operate as a power plant — only as a tritium consumer.

2. Neutron damage to first wall and divertor. ARC's compactness means more fusion power per unit of wall area. Multi-MW-yr/m² neutron fluence has never been survived by any material. The economic gating problem.

3. Tritium startup inventory. Each ARC startup requires ~10 kg of tritium.10 Global supply is decaying as CANDUs retire. By the time ARC-2 needs tritium, the supply may not exist outside breeding-from-ARC-1, which itself has not been validated.

The honest framing

SPARC will likely produce some plasma in 2026–2027. Whether it produces Q>1 at the predicted heating power, with predicted confinement, without disruption damage — that is the live question. ARC's TBR and first-wall problems are real and have no demonstration article between SPARC and a commercial plant. A reasonable Bayesian sees probability of commercial fusion-electricity by 2045 at maybe 35–50%. Higher than the LLM council's "well below 50%"; lower than the pitch's "imminent."

Five reasons CFS dominates the survival distribution

Why this company, not the others.

01 — Capital

~$3B raised

Roughly one-third of all private fusion capital ever deployed. The closest competitor (TAE) is at $1.3B with a harder physics problem. Capital concentration in CFS is increasing, not decreasing.

02 — Physics conservatism

Tokamak orthodoxy

CFS is doing the most validated approach (tokamak) with one well-defined improvement (better magnets). Helion, TAE, Zap, Pacific Fusion all carry physics risk CFS does not. The tradeoff is asymmetry: their upside is larger, their probability of physics success is materially lower.

03 — Customers

Plant pre-sold

Google 200 MW + Eni $1B+ = full 400 MW of ARC-1 under offtake. No competitor has a commercially-equivalent commitment. Helion's Microsoft deal is for a different reactor (Orion) on a more aggressive timeline.

04 — Governance signals

DOE, Bancel, DeepMind

Independent DOE validation of magnet performance (Sept 2025), Stéphane Bancel (Moderna) joining the board (Jan 2026), Google DeepMind embedded in operations. None of this is dispositive; collectively it is institutional ratification.

05 — Site infrastructure

Virginia siting locked

Chesterfield County zoning approved Sept 2025. Site sits on Dominion Energy land beside a retiring coal plant — meaning existing transmission interconnect. No competitor has cleared comparable siting friction.

06 — Geographic optionality

Japan in play

Talks with a 12-firm Japanese consortium led by Mitsui and Mitsubishi for a reactor in late 2030s/early 2040s. The TAM is global, not just Virginia.

The honest counter

None of this guarantees commercial success. It guarantees CFS survives long enough to find out. The relevant comparison is not "is CFS the best fusion company" — it's "if exactly one private fusion company reaches commercial revenue this decade, which one." On that question the evidence overwhelmingly favors CFS. If the right answer is "none of them," CFS's lead doesn't help — but no fusion bet helps in that scenario.

10 / 15COMMERCIAL
From physics demo to power-plant business

The customer side has gotten real.

Twelve months ago, fusion offtake was Microsoft's option deal with Helion plus a lot of optimism. Today, CFS specifically has converted its plant capacity into binding commercial commitments.

ARC-1 — Chesterfield County, VA

  • 400 MW grid-scale fusion plant. CFS finances, builds, owns, operates.
  • Google — 200 MW PPA signed June 2025; options on future plants. Google increased its equity stake concurrently.4
  • Eni — $1B+ PPA signed September 2025. Covers remaining capacity. Eni is also an investor.7
  • Site — James River Industrial Park. Owned by Dominion Energy. Adjacent to a retiring coal plant — existing grid interconnect.
  • Zoning — final approval September 2025.
  • Operational target — early 2030s, 20-year design life.

The deeper signal

Hyperscalers are not signing fusion PPAs because fusion will be cheap. Fusion will not be cheap at first power. First commercial electrons will plausibly be $150–300/MWh, vs. solar+storage at $50–100/MWh by 2035.

They are signing because data centers need firm, clean, 24/7 baseload in geographies and timeframes where renewables alone cannot deliver it. AI training clusters cannot intermittent-source. Fusion is one of three or four candidates (alongside SMR fission, enhanced geothermal, gas+CCS) for that load profile.

Translation: CFS is positioning into the AI infrastructure tailwind, not the bulk power market. The TAM is the firm-clean-baseload slot, not the global electricity market. That's a real industry, but it's smaller than the pitch implies.

The Mumgaard tell

Pre-2025, ARC was sequential after SPARC. Post-Google PPA, Mumgaard explicitly said the company can now "work more and more on ARC while we finish SPARC."12 ARC-1 land, zoning, offtakes, and engineering are happening in parallel with SPARC commissioning. If SPARC delivers Q>1 in 2027–2028, the company is not starting ARC — it is mid-execution. That compresses time-to-revenue by 2–3 years versus the LLM council's pre-mortem assumption.

11 / 15BEAR CASE
Steel-manned

How this loses.

A pre-mortem written from 2032, when the position has gone to ~zero. The failure modes that should give pause.

Path A — Death by TBR

SPARC achieves Q>1 in 2028 after a year's delay. Investors absorb the slip. Then in 2029–2030, ARC engineering design review reveals tritium breeding ratio of 0.95 in realistic geometry with engineering penetrations. ARC slips four years. CFS pivots to "ARC-0," a non-electricity-generating intermediate machine. Sophisticated investors mark CFS down 60–80%. Down round attempted. Strategic investor takes majority stake at distressed valuation. Original retail-adjacent secondary holders are wiped out via dilution and preference cascades.

Probability: 25–35%. This is the most likely failure path because TBR is the gating problem with the least demonstration data.

Path B — Death by market

Everything works. SPARC hits Q>11. ARC delivers electrons in 2032. And then nobody really wants it at the price fusion can deliver. Solar+storage crosses $40/MWh by 2035. Enhanced geothermal scales. SMR fission delivers. Behind-the-meter power matures. The Google offtake remains in force at a strike price that's 2–3× spot, but the company can't grow plant 2 because the market won't pay the premium at scale. CFS becomes a niche supplier of firm-clean-baseload to a small number of hyperscalers and military bases. Public market values it as a slow-growth utility. The IPO clears at 2–3× current valuation, not 10×.

Probability: 15–25%. The technology works; the economics don't.

Path C — Death by dilution

Everything works at the technical level. CFS delivers ARC-1, ARC-2, and the company is on its way to becoming the next-generation General Electric. And the 2026 secondary holder still captures only a fraction of the upside because the company raised $10B+ in additional equity over five rounds, plus convertible debt, plus project finance — and the secondary holder had no follow-on rights, no anti-dilution, and sat behind multiple liquidation preferences. At a 10x company-level return, the secondary buyer sees 3x net.

Probability: 30–40%, conditional on company success. This is the failure mode the LLM council named correctly and is harder to model than physics.

Path D — Death by tritium

CFS achieves Q>1 and ARC-1 reaches construction. ARC needs ~10 kg of tritium for startup; world supply is ~25 kg and declining as CANDU reactors retire.10 Romania's Cernavoda tritium facility comes online 2028 but is bottlenecked. ITER consumes its share. ARC-1 launches but ARC-2 cannot be fueled. The industry can't scale until tritium breeding is demonstrated at multi-plant level — which takes another 5+ years.

Probability: 10–20%. Real, but addressable through public-private partnership and policy changes already in motion at DOE.

Combined estimate of "money substantially lost or compounded below 1×": ~50–55%. Combined estimate of "money meaningfully made (3×+)": ~30–40%. Tail of "moonshot returns (10×+)": ~10–15%. Slide 13 lets you set these probabilities yourself.

What is actually being purchased

The investment structure.

We are not buying CFS shares directly. We are buying limited-partner interests in an SPV that holds CFS shares. The SPV's terms can capture more upside than the underlying loses. Read the fund docs, then re-read them.

5% carry materially better in any positive outcome. 20% upfront only "wins" if the deal goes to zero — and even then both lose. There is no scenario in which 20% upfront beats 5% carry on net return. The only argument for upfront is fund-level cash flow discipline.

What is still needed from the fund

  • Share class. Series B preferred? Common via secondary? Each has different liquidation preferences, voting, info rights.
  • Liquidation preferences senior to the SPV's position. If $1B of senior preferred sits ahead in the cap stack, the first $1B of an exit goes to those holders — not LPs.
  • SPV management fee schedule. Many SPVs have a 2% annual management fee on top of carry. That's another ~14% drag over 7 years.
  • Anti-dilution provisions. Almost certainly none at this entry tier.
  • Lockup mechanics. Tied to company liquidity event? IPO + 180 days?
  • Transfer restrictions. Can the SPV interest be exited on a secondary platform if needed?
  • Tax treatment. Pass-through? K-1? Long-term cap gains assuming held >1 year past liquidity event.
  • Co-invest right. If a future round opens, is there any right to participate?

The diligence that isn't fun but matters

This is the part the LLM council got right and is harder to model than physics. The company succeeding does not guarantee that this specific SPV interest succeeds. Read the fund agreement. Compute your effective ownership at exit under stated assumptions. Then re-read the liquidation-preference waterfall. If the fund will not share these documents, the answer is no — independent of how good CFS is.

14 / 15FRAMING
The question behind the question

What is actually being decided.

The right framing is not will fusion work, and not is CFS the best fusion company. Both have been answered — probably yes, and yes — to whatever degree is possible from public information. The actual decisions are these:

QUESTION 1

Is this a thesis already held?

Investors with portfolios already structured around variants of monetary debasement and AI-driven energy demand (gold, uranium, energy infrastructure, hard assets) are looking at CFS as the most concentrated single-name expression of that thesis available in the private market. If the thesis is right, refusing CFS on risk grounds is incoherent — the thesis already absorbs risk of this character. If the thesis is wrong, $25–100K is not the binding problem.

QUESTION 2

Is the SPV structure tolerable?

A $25K position in a high-quality direct round ≠ a $25K LP interest in an SPV with carry, possible management fees, secondary-platform markup, no information rights, and uncertain liquidation preferences. The vehicle can capture more upside than the underlying loses. Until fund docs are in hand, this question cannot be answered. This is a literal gating question, not rhetorical.

QUESTION 3

Is this incremental risk capital?

$25K (Investor A) is <1% of plausible household NW for the use case modeled here. $25–100K (Investor B) requires an independent answer. This must be capital that, at zero, does not change either lifestyle or other financial commitments. If it would, the answer is no, regardless of how good CFS is. The 5–10 year illiquidity is the binding constraint, not the principal.

QUESTION 4

Is the asymmetry real for this entry?

A 2026 secondary buyer is not getting Series B2 economics. Per the calculator: at base-case 50% dilution to a $50B exit, gross is ~3.6×, net (5% carry) is ~3.4×. Not 50× lottery. Not 1.5× nothingburger. An honest probability-weighted expected value of ~2.8× over 7 years is plausible — that's a ~16% IRR on illiquid speculative capital. Below top-quartile private equity benchmarks. Above index funds. Defensible, not heroic.

15 / 15RECOMMENDATION
Explicit, not coy

Recommendation.

Conditional yes for both investors, contingent on four gates. If any gate fails, the answer is no — independent of how attractive CFS is in the abstract.

GATE 1

Read the actual SPV terms.

Carry rate. Management fee. Share class. Liquidation preferences senior to the SPV. Lockup. Information rights. If the fund will not share these documents in writing before commitment, decline. If they will, model a worst-case waterfall before signing.

GATE 2

Verify the implied valuation at SPV entry.

CFS B2 post-money was not publicly disclosed. Market estimates put it at $6–9B. Some pre-IPO secondary platforms quote share prices implying 1.5–2× the last primary round. If the SPV is buying at a markup over Series B2, that markup is dead-weight loss for the LP. Confirm the entry price and the implied valuation.

GATE 3

Confirm sizing.

For Investor A: $25K is appropriate as a thesis expression alongside existing macro positioning. For Investor B: $25K is a dip; $100K is a different conversation. If household NW supports it without affecting other commitments, $50–75K is defensible. If $100K would require any compromise on illiquidity tolerance, take the lower bound.

GATE 4

Choose 5% carry over 20% upfront if both are offered.

There is no realistic outcome distribution in which 20% upfront beats 5% carry on net return. The carry structure leaves more upside conditional on success and equivalent downside conditional on failure. The only argument for upfront is fund liquidity discipline — irrelevant to LPs.

What this is not

Not investment advice. Not a recommendation to commit capital before reading fund docs. Not certainty about CFS's commercial success — probability estimated at 35–50% by 2045. Not certainty about the secondary buyer's share of upside — estimated at 30–60% of company-level returns, depending on dilution and SPV terms.

What this is

A coherent, modestly-sized expression of an existing thesis, in the most credible private-market vehicle for it, with a probability-weighted expected return that is defensible if the gates above clear. If they don't clear, walking away is also a complete answer.
